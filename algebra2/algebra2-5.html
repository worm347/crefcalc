Topic A: Exponential Functions & Sequences

01–03 — Lessons 1–3

Exponential Growth & Decay

f(x) = a·bˣ models situations that multiply repeatedly.

  • a: initial value (y-intercept, f(0) = a).
  • b > 1: growth factor — increases exponentially.
  • 0 < b < 1: decay factor — decreases toward zero.
  • Continuous growth: f(t) = ae^(rt), base e ≈ 2.718.
  • Doubling time: when f(t) = 2a → solve 2 = b^t.
Example: $1,000 at 5% annual growth: f(t) = 1000·(1.05)ᵗ. After 10 years: f(10) ≈ $1,629.

04–05 — Lessons 4–5

Geometric Sequences & Series

A geometric sequence multiplies by a constant ratio r each term.

  • General term: aₙ = a₁ · r^(n−1).
  • Common ratio: r = aₙ₊₁/aₙ.
  • Finite sum: Sₙ = a₁(1 − rⁿ)/(1 − r), r ≠ 1.
  • Infinite sum (|r|<1): S = a₁/(1 − r).
Sequence: 3, 6, 12, 24 … → a₁ = 3, r = 2 → a₅ = 3·2⁴ = 48

06–07 — Lessons 6–7

Logarithms as Inverses of Exponentials

Logarithms undo exponentiation. log_b(x) = y means b^y = x.

  • Common log: log(x) = log₁₀(x).
  • Natural log: ln(x) = log_e(x).
  • Inverse relationship: b^(log_b(x)) = x and log_b(b^x) = x.
  • Domain of log: x > 0. Range: all reals.
log₂(8) = 3 because 2³ = 8.  ·  ln(e⁵) = 5.

08–10 — Lessons 8–10

Properties of Logarithms

  • Product: log_b(MN) = log_b(M) + log_b(N).
  • Quotient: log_b(M/N) = log_b(M) − log_b(N).
  • Power: log_b(Mⁿ) = n·log_b(M).
  • Change of base: log_b(x) = log(x)/log(b).
  • Use to expand or condense logarithmic expressions.
log(x²y/z) = 2log(x) + log(y) − log(z)

11–14 — Lessons 11–14

Solving Exponential & Logarithmic Equations

Use logarithms to solve equations where the variable is in the exponent.

  • To solve bˣ = c: take log of both sides → x = log(c)/log(b).
  • To solve log_b(x) = c: rewrite as b^c = x.
  • Check: for log equations, verify the argument > 0.
  • Use ln when working with base e.
3ˣ = 20 → x = log(20)/log(3) = ln(20)/ln(3) ≈ 2.73

15–16 — Lessons 15–16

Exponential Modeling in Context

Apply exponential and log functions to real-world growth, decay, and finance.

  • Compound interest: A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt).
  • Continuous compounding: A = Pe^(rt).
  • Half-life: f(t) = a·(0.5)^(t/h), h = half-life period.
  • Use logs to find when a quantity reaches a specific value.
Carbon-14 half-life ≈ 5,730 yr. After 11,460 yr (2 half-lives): 1/4 remains.
