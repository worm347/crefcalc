01–02 — Lessons 1–2

Degree, Leading Term & End Behavior

The degree and leading coefficient determine how a polynomial behaves at the far left and right.

  • Degree: highest exponent in the polynomial.
  • Leading coefficient: coefficient of the highest-degree term.
  • Even degree, + leading: both ends go up (↑↑).
  • Even degree, − leading: both ends go down (↓↓).
  • Odd degree, + leading: left down, right up (↓↑).
  • Odd degree, − leading: left up, right down (↑↓).
f(x) = −2x³ + 5x → degree 3 (odd), leading −2 (negative) → ↑↓ end behavior.

03–05 — Lessons 3–5

Zeros, Multiplicity & Graphs

The zeros of a polynomial correspond to x-intercepts. Multiplicity affects how the graph behaves at each zero.

  • Zero of multiplicity 1: graph crosses the x-axis.
  • Zero of multiplicity 2 (even): graph touches and bounces off x-axis.
  • Zero of multiplicity 3 (odd > 1): graph crosses but flattens.
  • Max # of zeros = degree of the polynomial.
  • Use factored form to identify zeros: f(x) = a(x−r₁)(x−r₂)…
f(x) = (x−2)²(x+1) → zero at x=2 (mult. 2, bounce), x=−1 (mult. 1, cross).

06–07 — Lessons 6–7

Adding, Subtracting & Multiplying Polynomials

Polynomials are closed under addition, subtraction, and multiplication — the result is always a polynomial.

  • Add/Subtract: combine like terms (same degree).
  • Multiply: use the distributive property (FOIL for two binomials, then for larger).
  • Degree of product = sum of degrees of the two polynomials.
(x+2)(x²−x+3) = x³−x²+3x + 2x²−2x+6 = x³ + x² + x + 6

08–09 — Lessons 8–9

Polynomial Long Division

Divide a polynomial by another using a process similar to numeric long division.

  • Dividend ÷ Divisor = Quotient + Remainder/Divisor
  • Step 1: Divide the leading term of the dividend by the leading term of the divisor.
  • Step 2: Multiply, subtract, bring down.
  • Step 3: Repeat until the remainder has lower degree than the divisor.
  • Synthetic division: shortcut when dividing by (x − c).
(x³ − 2x + 4) ÷ (x − 1) → quotient x² + x − 1, remainder 3.

Topic C: Polynomial Theorems & Identities

Lessons 10–14 · Remainder theorem, factor theorem, and polynomial identities.

10–12 — Lessons 10–12

Remainder & Factor Theorems

  • Remainder Theorem: when p(x) is divided by (x − c), the remainder = p(c).
  • Factor Theorem: (x − c) is a factor of p(x) if and only if p(c) = 0.
  • Use to test potential zeros quickly without full division.
  • Rational Root Theorem: possible rational zeros are ±(factors of constant) / (factors of leading coefficient).
p(x) = x³ − 6x + 4. Test x = 2: p(2) = 8 − 12 + 4 = 0 → (x−2) is a factor.

13–14 — Lessons 13–14

Polynomial Identities

Polynomial identities are equations that are true for all values of the variable.

  • (a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b²
  • (a − b)² = a² − 2ab + b²
  • (a + b)(a − b) = a² − b² (difference of squares)
  • (a + b)³ = a³ + 3a²b + 3ab² + b³
  • Use identities to factor, simplify, and solve problems efficiently.
Factor: x² − 25 = (x+5)(x−5) using difference of squares identity.
