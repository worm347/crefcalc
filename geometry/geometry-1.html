01–03 — Lessons 1–3

Basic Constructions

Geometric constructions use only a compass (for circles/arcs) and a straightedge (for lines — no measurements).

  • Copy a segment: use compass to transfer length.
  • Copy an angle: reproduce the angle's arc and chord at a new vertex.
  • Perpendicular bisector: find midpoint of segment; line perpendicular to it.
  • Angle bisector: divide an angle into two equal halves.
  • Constructions are exact — no measuring with rulers or protractors.
Perpendicular bisector of AB: set compass > AB/2, draw arcs from A and B, connect the intersections.

Circle Constructions & Special Figures

  • Inscribed circle: tangent to all sides of a triangle (incircle) — constructed using angle bisectors.
  • Circumscribed circle: passes through all vertices (circumcircle) — constructed using perpendicular bisectors.
  • Equilateral triangle: set compass = side length, draw arcs from endpoints.
  • Regular hexagon: radius = side length, mark 6 points around center circle.
  • Square: use perpendicular bisectors and circle.
To construct an equilateral triangle: draw segment AB, arc from A (radius AB), arc from B — their intersection is C.

07–09 — Lessons 7–9

Introduction to Geometric Proof

Geometric proof uses definitions, postulates, and previously proven theorems to establish that a statement is always true.

  • Given: information you are told is true.
  • Prove: the statement you must show is true.
  • Two-column proof: statements on left, reasons on right.
  • Reasons: definition, postulate, theorem, or given.
  • Each step follows logically from what came before.
Given: AB ≅ CD, CD ≅ EF. Prove: AB ≅ EF. Reason: Transitive Property of Congruence.

Angle Relationships & Proofs

  • Vertical angles: congruent. (Formed by two intersecting lines, across from each other.)
  • Linear pair: supplementary (add to 180°).
  • Complementary: two angles adding to 90°.
  • Transversal + parallel lines: alternate interior angles ≅, corresponding angles ≅, co-interior angles supplementary.
  • Use these theorems as reasons in proofs.
Given: ∠1 and ∠2 are vertical angles. Prove: ∠1 ≅ ∠2. Reason: Vertical Angles Theorem.

Topic C: Rigid Motions

Lessons 13–19 · Translations, reflections, rotations, and composites.

13–15 — Lessons 13–15

Translations & Reflections

  • Rigid motion (isometry): a transformation that preserves distance and angle measure.
  • Translation: slides a figure. Vector notation (a, b): move right a, up b.
  • Reflection: flips a figure over a line of reflection.
  • Reflect over x-axis: (x, y) → (x, −y).
  • Reflect over y-axis: (x, y) → (−x, y).
  • Reflect over y = x: (x, y) → (y, x).
Point A(3, 5) reflected over x-axis → A'(3, −5). Reflected over y-axis → A'(−3, 5).

16–19 — Lessons 16–19

Rotations & Composition

  • Rotation: turns a figure around a center point by an angle.
  • Rotate 90° CCW about origin: (x, y) → (−y, x).
  • Rotate 180° about origin: (x, y) → (−x, −y).
  • Rotate 270° CCW (= 90° CW): (x, y) → (y, −x).
  • Composition: apply two or more transformations in sequence.
  • A glide reflection = translation + reflection.
Rotate (2, 3) by 90° CCW about origin: (2, 3) → (−3, 2).
